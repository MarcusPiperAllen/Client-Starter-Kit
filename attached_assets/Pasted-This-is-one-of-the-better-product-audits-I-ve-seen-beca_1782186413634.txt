This is one of the better product audits I've seen because it wasn't focused on code quality—it was focused on whether the product is becoming what you intended.

The sentence that jumps out at me is:

> "LaunchFrame is genuinely on the path toward the vision." 

A few months ago, LaunchFrame was basically:

```text
Form
→ Template
→ Output
```

Today it's:

```text
Brain Dump
→ Analysis
→ Intent Extraction
→ Gap Detection
→ Structured Intent
→ Prompt
```

That's a very different product.

---

## What I think the audit got exactly right

This part:

> "The missing step is asking." 

I think that's the entire future of LaunchFrame.

Not:

❌ Better HTML

❌ Better CSS

❌ More starter code

❌ More dropdowns

But:

✅ Better questions

The moment I read:

> "The suggestions are presented as a static list." 

I immediately thought:

"Yep. That's the bottleneck."

Because right now LaunchFrame does:

```text
User talks
↓
LaunchFrame thinks
↓
LaunchFrame outputs
```

But a strategist does:

```text
User talks
↓
Strategist thinks
↓
Strategist asks
↓
User clarifies
↓
Strategist asks
↓
User clarifies
↓
Final recommendation
```

---

## The biggest hidden insight

The auditor accidentally described a future feature without realizing it.

Look at these:

> User permissions are not defined

> Client access is unclear

> Pricing model is unclear

Those aren't suggestions.

Those are questions.

The system already knows what it wants to ask.

The UI just doesn't let it ask them.

---

## What I would NOT do next

I would not immediately build:

* user accounts
* project history
* team collaboration
* vector databases
* memory systems

Those are all nice.

None of them make the core product smarter.

---

## What I WOULD do next

I would write a planning prompt for a feature called:

### Intent Resolution Layer

New flow:

```text
Brain Dump
↓
Intent Miner
↓
Extracted Intent
↓
Intent Resolution Layer
↓
Final Prompt
```

Example:

User dumps BriefStack.

LaunchFrame responds:

> I understand the project.
>
> Before I build the final prompt, I need 3 decisions.

Question 1:

> Will clients have their own login portal?

Question 2:

> Should subscriptions be part of MVP or future phase?

Question 3:

> Should agency teams share workspaces?

User answers.

Prompt improves dramatically.

---

## Another thing the audit exposed

The auditor gave:

> Product Vision Score: 6.5 / 10 

At first glance that sounds low.

I actually think that's encouraging.

Why?

Because the score wasn't dragged down by:

* extraction
* understanding
* prompt quality

It was dragged down by:

* lack of conversation
* lack of refinement
* lack of interaction

Those are easier problems than teaching the system to understand projects.

---

## My recommendation

I would not immediately hand Claude a coding task.

I would spend one planning cycle defining:

### "What exactly happens during the Intent Resolution Layer?"

Questions like:

* How many questions?
* How are they ranked?
* Can users skip them?
* Does the form update automatically?
* Does the final prompt include the answers?
* How does Auto-Build behave?

Because the audit is basically telling us:

> "The miner works. The prompt works. The missing piece is the discussion between them." 

And that feels very aligned with the product vision you've been describing for months:

> "I dump everything I know. The system helps me think. Then it gives me a better starter prompt than I could have created myself."

That's the next frontier for LaunchFrame.
